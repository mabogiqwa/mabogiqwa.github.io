Scalars, Vectors, Matrices, and Tensors

A scalar can be viewed as a real number. Scalars are written in italics. We also denote them in lowercase variable style forms. For example, we might say that let \(s \in \mathbb{R} \) be the slope of a line while defining a real-valued scalar. A scalar can also be seen as a matrix with a single entry which would imply that \(a = a^T \).

A vector is an array of numbers. Numbers that arranged in order. Vectors are denoted in a lowercase bold typeface. The first element of a vector x is \(x_1 \), the second element is \( x_2 \) and so on. You could say that the first element of a vector is denoted by it's name in italic typeface with a subscript. Example of a vector is:

x\(\hspace{0.5em} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ . \\ . \\ x_n \end{bmatrix} \)

We also have that \(x_{-1} \) denotes a vector that contains all elements of x excerpt \( x_{1} \). Let \(S = \{1, 3, 6 \} \) then \( x_{-S} \) is the vector that denotes all the elements of \( x \) excerpt 1, 3, and 6.

if \(\hspace{0.3em}\) x \(\hspace{0.4em} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \hspace{0.3em} \) then \( \hspace{0.4em} x_{-S} \hspace{0.3em}\) = \(\hspace{0.3em} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \)

A matrix can be viewed as a 2-dimensional version of a matrix in that each element is identified by two indices instead of one. Matrices are denoted using uppercase variable names with a bold typeface such as A. A matrix with height \(m \) and width \(n \) is denoted as \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \). Example of a square matrix is:

A = \(\begin{bmatrix} a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & a_{1,3} \\ a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & a_{2,3} \\ a_{3,1} & a_{3,2} & a_{3,3} \end{bmatrix} \) where \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3} \)

The transpose of some matrix can be seen as the flipping of a matrix across the main diagonal.

A = \(\begin{bmatrix} a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} \\ a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} \\ a_{3,1} & a_{3,2} \end{bmatrix} \hspace{0.5em} \Rightarrow \hspace{0,5em} A^T = \begin{bmatrix} a_{1,1} & a_{2,1} & a_{3,1} \\ a_{1,2} & a_{2,2} & a_{3,2} \end{bmatrix} \)

You could also interpret the transpose as in \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \) then \(A^{T} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m} \) and that each row vector becomes a column vector when transposed.

As we extend to the 3-dimensional realm, we'll need an array with more than two axes. In the general case, an array of numbers arranged in a rectangular grid with a variable number of axes is known as a tensor. A tensor is denoted A. We identify a certain element of A at coordinates \((i, j, k) \) by writing \(A_{i, j, k} \). Operations such as transposition also apply to tensors. An example of a tensor is:

\[ A = \left( \left( \begin{matrix} a_{1,1,1} & a_{1,1,2} & a_{1,1,3} \\ a_{1,2,1} & a_{1,2,2} & a_{1,2,3} \\ a_{1,3,1} & a_{1,3,2} & a_{1,3,3} \end{matrix} \right), \left( \begin{matrix} a_{2,1,1} & a_{2,1,2} & a_{2,1,3} \\ a_{2,2,1} & a_{2,2,2} & a_{2,2,3} \\ a_{2,3,1} & a_{2,3,2} & a_{2,3,3} \end{matrix} \right), \left( \begin{matrix} a_{3,1,1} & a_{3,1,2} & a_{3,1,3} \\ a_{3,2,1} & a_{3,2,2} & a_{3,2,3} \\ a_{3,3,1} & a_{3,3,2} & a_{3,3,3} \end{matrix} \right) \right) \]

Things to note:

Matrix addition works iff the matrices being added have the same shape. We add them by adding their corresponding elements. \(C = A + B \) where \(C_{i, j} = A_{i, j} + B_{i, j} \).

Matrix multiplication works iff we have matrices \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \) and \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p} \). The width of \( A \) must be equal to the height of \(B \) for the product \(AB \) to exist.

It is possible to add a matrix and a vector which yield another matrix: \(C = A + b \) where \(C_{i,j} = A_{i,j} + b_j \). What happens in this operation is that vector \(b\) is added to each row of matrix A. This operation is termed broadcasting. For instance:

Let \(\hspace{0.3em}\)A = \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9\end{bmatrix} \) and \(\hspace{0.2em} \) x = \(\begin{bmatrix} 10 & 20 & 30\end{bmatrix} \)

A + x = \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9\end{bmatrix} \ + \begin{bmatrix} 10 & 20 & 30 \\ 10 & 20 & 30 \\ 10 & 20 & 30\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}11 & 22 & 33 \\ 14 & 25 & 36 \\ 17 & 28 & 39\end{bmatrix}\)

Multiplying Matrices and Vectors

The matrix product of matrices A and B is a third matrix C. In order for the product to be defined A must have the same number of columns as B has rows. If we given that A has the shape \(m \times n \) and B has the shape \(n \times p \), then C has the shape \(m \times p \). The matrix product is written as,

\(C = AB \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.1)}\)

while the product operation is defined by,

\(C_{i,j} = \sum_{k}A_{i,k}B_{k,j} \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.2)}\)

An example of matrix multiplication is:

Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \\3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\)

\[ \begin{aligned} AB &= \begin{bmatrix}2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \\[8pt] &= \begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \\[8pt] &= \begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 \\ 4 & 8\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}9 & 12 \\ 15 & 20\end{bmatrix} \\[8pt] &= \begin{bmatrix} 11 & 16 \\ 19 & 28\end{bmatrix} \end{aligned} \]

It is important to note that the standard product is not just a matrix that contains the product of individual corresponding elements. If we were to conduct such an operation, it would be called a Hadamard product which is denoted as \(A \odot B \).

The dot product between two vector x and y which has the same dimensions is the matrix product \(x^{T}y\).

Here are a couple of algebraic axioms related to matrix multiplication:

\[ \begin{aligned} A(B + C) &= AB + AC \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.3)} \\ A(BC) &= (AB)C \hspace{4.2em} \textcolor{red}{(2.4)} \\ (AB)^{T} &= B^{T}A^{T} \hspace{4.8em} \textcolor{red}{(2.5)} \end{aligned} \]

Remember that \(AB \neq BA \) since matrix multiplication is not commutative. We however have commutativity between the dot product of two vectors. So

\( x^{T}y = y^{T}x \) \(\hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.6)} \)

A system of linear equations is denoted as:

\(\hspace{-5em} Ax = b \hspace{10em} \textcolor{red}{(2.7)}\)

where \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) is a given matrix, \(b \in \mathbb{R}^{m}\) is a given vector and \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n \) is the vector of unknown variable we would like to solve for. We would like to find the value for each \(x_i \) in \(x \).
We can express \(\textcolor{red}{2.7}\) as:

\[ \begin{aligned} A_{1},:x &= b_{1} \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.8)} \\ A_{2},:x &= b_{2} \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.9)} \\ \text{. . .} \\ A_{m},:x &= b_{m} \hspace{2.6em} \textcolor{red}{(2.10)} \end{aligned} \]

and more compactly as

\[ \begin{aligned} A_{1,1}x_1 + A_{1,2}x_2 + \dots + A_{1,n}x_n &= b_1 \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.11)} \\ A_{2,1}x_1 + A_{2,2}x_2 + \dots + A_{2,n}x_n &= b_2 \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.12)} \\ \dots \hspace{5em} \\ A_{m,1}x_1 + A_{m,2}x_2 + \dots + A_{m,n}x_n &= b_m \hspace{2.7em} \textcolor{red}{(2.13)} \\ \end{aligned} \]

An example of an identity matrix is:

\(I_3 = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & 0 \\0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}\)

Identity and Inverse Matrices

Matrix inversion allows us to solve linear system in the form \(\textcolor{red}{2.7}\) analytically for many values of A and \(b\).

To fully describe matrix inversion, we also need to define the concept of an identity matrix. An identiy matrix is a matrix that does not enact any change to any matrix it is multiplied by. The identity matrix which preserves \(n \)-dimensional vectors is denoted as \(I_n\). \(I_n \in \mathbb{R}^{n}\) and,

\(\forall x \in \mathbb{R}^{n}, I_n x = x\)

An identity matrix can be recognized by that beside the main diagonal which has 1, other entries are 0 and further more, it is a square matrix which implies that the width and height of the matrix are equivalent.

The inverse of some matrix A is denoted \(A^{-1}\) and the following is true that;

\(A^{-1}A = I_n \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.14)}\)

Equation \(\textcolor{red}{2.7}\) can be solved in the following way:

\[ \begin{aligned} Ax &= b \\ A^{-1}Ax &= A^{-1}b \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.15)} \\ I_{n}x &= A^{-1}b \hspace{3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.16)} \end{aligned} \]

\(\hspace{-1em}\)Hence \(\hspace{2.5em} x = A^{-1}b \hspace{3.3em} \textcolor{red}{(2.17)}\)

This way of finding the coefficient matrix is of course dependent on us finding \(A^{-1}\) which gets more computationally expensive process as the dimensions of some matrix A scale.

An example of the standard product of a matrix A and it's inverse \(A^{-1}\):

Let A = \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \\1 & 1 & 2 \\-1 & 2 & 1\end{bmatrix}\), then \(A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & -\frac{5}{12} \\ \frac{1}{4} & 0 & \frac{1}{3} \\ -\frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{12} \end{bmatrix}\)

\(\hspace{-12em}A.A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \\1 & 1 & 2 \\-1 & 2 & 1\end{bmatrix}.\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & -\frac{5}{12} \\ \frac{1}{4} & 0 & \frac{1}{3} \\ -\frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{12} \end{bmatrix}\)
\(\hspace{4em} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\\-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & -\frac{5}{12}\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\frac{1}{4} & 0 & \frac{1}{3}\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}-1\\2\\1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}-\frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{12}\end{bmatrix}\) \(\hspace{1em} = \begin{bmatrix}\frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & -\frac{5}{12}\\ \frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & -\frac{5}{12} \\ -\frac{1}{4} & -\frac{1}{3} & \frac{5}{12}\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}\frac{1}{2} & 0 & \frac{2}{3}\\ \frac{1}{4} & 0 & \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & 0 & \frac{2}{3}\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{4} & -\frac{1}{3} & -\frac{1}{12} \\ -\frac{1}{2} & \frac{2}{3} & \frac{1}{6} \\ -\frac{1}{4} & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{12}\end{bmatrix}\)

\(\hspace{-17.8em} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & \sim 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & \sim 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

Note that the resulting identity matrix has approximate entries since I analytically found \(A^{-1}\). Using Octave/Matlab to find the inverse of A will result in \(AA^{-1}\) being an identity matrix with exact values.

Linear Dependence and Span

In order for \(A^{-1}\) to exist, \(\textcolor{red}{2.11}\) must have one solution for every value of \(b\). It is also possible that a system of equations has no solution or infinitely many solutions for some values of \(b\).

To analyze how many solutions an equation has, we can think of the columns of A as specifying the different directions we can travel from the origin, and in this way we can determine how many ways there are of reaching \(b\). Within this point of view, each element of \(x\) specifies how far we should travel in each of the directions and each \(x_i\) indicating how far we should move in the direction of column \(i\):

\(Ax = \sum_{i}x_{i}A_{:,i}\)

This kind of operation is normally called a linear combination. A linear combination is some set of vectors \(\{v^{(1)},...,v^{(n)}\}\) which we get by multiplying each vector \(v^{(i)}\) by a corresponding scalar coefficient and adding the results:

\(\sum_{i}c_{i}v^{(i)}\)

The span of a set of vectors refers to the set of all possible linear combinations from the said set of vectors.

Figuring out whether \(Ax = b\) has a solution comes to testing whether \(b\) exists in the span of the columns of \(A\). This type of span is known as the column space or range of A.
