Inside the Black Box: How Neural Networks Learn

It's been a desire of the ages to enable computers to think. This desire dates back at least to times of Ancient Greece. Figures such as Galatea, Talos, and Pandora in Greek tales may be regarded as artificial life.

When programmable computers were first created, people had already wondered whether we could make machines capable of conscious thought. This was a hundred years before Ada Lovelace, 1842 and Charles Babbage designed the analytical engine, a machine capable of performing mathematical calculations. Nowadays, computational intelligence/AI(Artificial Intelligence) is a thriving field that is almost ubiquitous in most of the technology we interact with. We look to intelligent software to automate routine labor, understand speech, analyze images, make diagnoses in medicine, and support basic scientific research.

During the early days of it's inception, the field of AI took on challenges which were difficult for humans. Most of these tasks could be solved using algorithms which are a finite set of instructions that when given an input are supposed to generate an output. These tasks were in areas such mathematical computation, game-playing, symbolic logic and theorem proving, rule-based decision making and so on. Ironically, the things that were simple to us proved to be difficult to be performed by AI algorithms at the time. Things such as recognizing the nuance in color, recognizing human faces, and understanding natural language.

A solution to problems involving intuitive tasks is Deep Learning. Deep is a subset of Machine Learning that enables computers to learn from experience(data) and get an understanding of the world in the form of hierarchical concepts. Since deep learning allows computers to get better at a task from experience, it is not necessary to have a human operator who hard-codes knowledge/concepts. Machine Learning can be defined as enabling computers to get better at a task by acquiring patterns in data without explicit programming. Machine Learning has enabled computers to tackle real-world problems and make decisions that seem to be subjective.

Logistic regression, a machine learning algorithm can determine whether to recommend a cesarean delivery. Naive Bayes, a family of ML algorithms can be used to separate emails from valid sources and spam. How these relatively simple algorithms perform depends on the representation of data. For instance, when the logistic regression algorithm predicts probabilities based on learned correlations on whether to carry out a cesarean delivery or not. It does not directly examine the patient. It instead waits for the necessary input from the doctor which comes in the form of features. Features could be things such as fetal position, fetal weight, fetal distress, and so on. Logistic regression then learns how these features correlate with various outcomes. Furthermore, logistic regression can also be used in fields such as law to classify documents as either contracts, case law or statutes.
