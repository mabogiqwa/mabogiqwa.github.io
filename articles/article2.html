Inside the Black Box: How Neural Networks Learn

It's been a desire of the ages to enable computers to think. This desire dates back at least to times of Ancient Greece. Figures such as Galatea, Talos, and Pandora in Greek tales may be regarded as artificial life.

When programmable computers were first created, people had already wondered whether we could make machines capable of conscious thought. This was a hundred years before Ada Lovelace, 1842 and Charles Babbage designed the analytical engine, a machine capable of performing mathematical calculations. Nowadays, computational intelligence/AI(Artificial Intelligence) is a thriving field that is almost ubiquitous in most of the technology we interact with. We look to intelligent software to automate routine labor, understand speech, analyze images, make diagnoses in medicine, and support basic scientific research.

During the early days of it's inception, the field of AI took on challenges which were difficult for humans. Most of these tasks could be solved using algorithms which are a finite set of instructions that when given an input are supposed to generate an output. These tasks were in areas such mathematical computation, game-playing, symbolic logic and theorem proving, rule-based decision making and so on. Ironically, the things that were simple to us proved to be difficult to be performed by AI algorithms at the time. Things such as recognizing the nuance in color, recognizing human faces, and understanding natural language.

A solution to problems involving intuitive tasks is Deep Learning. Deep is a subset of Machine Learning that enables computers to learn from experience(data) and get an understanding of the world in the form of hierarchical concepts. Since deep learning allows computers to get better at a task from experience, it is not necessary to have a human operator who hard-codes knowledge/concepts. Machine Learning can be defined as enabling computers to get better at a task by acquiring patterns in data without explicit programming. Machine Learning has enabled computers to tackle real-world problems and make decisions that seem to be subjective.

Logistic regression, a machine learning algorithm can determine whether to recommend a cesarean delivery. Naive Bayes, a family of ML algorithms can be used to separate emails from valid sources and spam. How these relatively simple algorithms perform depends on the representation of data. For instance, when the logistic regression algorithm predicts probabilities based on learned correlations on whether to carry out a cesarean delivery or not. It does not directly examine the patient. It instead waits for the necessary input from the doctor which comes in the form of features. Features could be things such as fetal position, fetal weight, fetal distress, and so on. Logistic regression then learns how these features correlate with various outcomes. Furthermore, logistic regression can also be used in a field such as law to classify documents as either contracts, case law or statutes.

Representation of data is a phenomenon that is present in computer science and daily life. In computer science, operations such as search happen much more efficiently when data collection is structured and indexed astutely. For instance, if we were to successfully/unsuccessfully search the set {1,3,5,6,9,2} using a sequential search, the time complexity would be \(O(n)\) whereas if we were to sort the set in ascending order. Using a binary search, a successful/unsuccessful search would have a time complexity of \(O(log_{2}n)\). The importance of the correct representation in everyday life can be seen in that people find it easier to algebraically manipulate Hindu-Arabic numerals compared to Roman numerals. Choosing the right representation is super important as it also expands into computational aspects such as storage efficiency, computational speed, and interpretability.

Choosing the right features for an ML task can be difficult. If we wanted to write a program to discern cars in images, the first thing we might look out for is that cars have wheels. So we might select the presence of wheels as a feature. We would however encounter the difficulty of having to describe wheels in terms of pixel values. The concept of a wheel is simple to describe geometrically but the challenge lies in describing the various forms it may be in images. For instance, the wheel's form may be distorted or obscured by things such as shadows falling on the wheel, the sun glaring off the rims of the wheel, the fender of the wheel, and many other various phenomena.

One way to side-step the problem of having to choose appropriate representations for a task is to use machine learning to not only map the representation to output but also to find the representation itself. The approach of finding the representations along with the outputs is called representation learning. In most cases, learned representations result in a better performance than if we were to hand-pick representations. One of the key aspects of representation learning is that it allows AI systems to adapt much quicker to new tasks with little outside intervention. Representation learning comes in handy as it removes the immense human labor needed to select features for a complex task.

A perfect example of a representation algorithm is the auto-encoder. An auto-encoder is a mix of an encoder function and a decoder function. How it works is that the encoder converts raw input data into a different representation which is in a lower dimension. Raw data could be things such as images, videos, or audio which are converted into some \(n\)-dimensional vector that only keeps key features. A decoder inverts the actions of an encoder in that it transforms the lower dimensional representation into its original format. Auto-encoders are trained using unsupervised learning to retain as much information as they can.

When we are designing features, we usually want to discern and separate the various influences-known as factors of variation-that can be used to explain the observed data. These influences/factors are mostly quantities that cannot be directly observed. They are often phenomena that go unobserved in the physical world but still have an effect on observable quantities. They are constructs of the human mind which of course are abstractions of the causes of the observed data and help us make sense of the rich variability of factors that exist in data. When we examine a speech recording, factors of variation may have things such as the speaker's mood, their accent, age, and gender. Another instance would be when we are analysing market data, factors of variation could be things such as price movements, quantity of goods sold or produced, consumer behavior, and market trends.
